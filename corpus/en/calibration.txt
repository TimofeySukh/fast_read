On the first morning of the journey the station was still half asleep. The lamps on the platform had not yet been turned off, and the windows of the waiting room glowed with a tired yellow light. A porter was dragging a cart across the stone floor, and the wheels rattled so loudly that every sleepy traveler turned to look.

A woman in a dark coat stood near the timetable and checked the same line again and again, as if the departure hour might change simply because she wished it to. An old man beside her held a paper cup of coffee in both hands, warming his fingers before every cautious sip. Somewhere farther down the platform a child laughed, and the sound seemed almost too cheerful for the cold blue dawn.

When the train finally arrived, it did not rush in like a dramatic machine from an adventure story. It rolled forward slowly, breathing steam and metal, patient and heavy, as though it had already crossed half the continent and was prepared to cross the other half as well. Doors opened one after another. People stepped aside, searched for carriage numbers, lifted bags, called short instructions to one another, and disappeared inside.

The narrator of this small scene would probably say that nothing remarkable happened there at all. No one made a speech. No one missed the train by a second. No secret letter changed hands. And yet that quiet departure stayed in memory longer than many louder events, perhaps because every movement on the platform seemed full of intention.

A journey often begins before a person sits down by the window. It begins when the ticket is folded and put into a pocket, when the station clock is checked for the third time, when a stranger asks whether this is the right track, and when the traveler feels that curious mixture of impatience and doubt that belongs only to departures.

By the time the train left the city behind, the first sunlight had reached the roofs near the freight yard. The passengers settled in, the porter had vanished from sight, and the platform became only a memory. But for a little while the rhythm of the wheels kept echoing the same thought: the day has started, the road is open, and whatever comes next has already begun.

The Frog's breath almost stopped from the terrible height to which she had been lifted. Besides, the ducks flew unevenly and tugged at the stick. The poor frog swung in the air like a paper puppet and clenched her jaws with all her might so as not to let go and fall to the ground. Soon, however, she grew used to her position and even began to look around. Fields, meadows, rivers, and mountains slipped quickly by beneath her. It was hard for her to see well, since she hung from the stick looking backward and a little upward, but she still saw enough to feel delighted and proud.

"What an excellent idea I had," she thought to herself.

The ducks flying behind the leading pair shouted their praise.

"Our frog has an astonishingly clever head," they said. "There are not many as clever as she is, even among ducks."

The Frog could hardly stop herself from thanking them, but she remembered in time that if she opened her mouth she would fall from a frightful height. So she only clenched her jaws more tightly and resolved to endure.

In this way she traveled for a whole day. The ducks carrying her changed places in flight, skillfully catching the stick. It was very frightening. More than once the Frog nearly croaked from terror, but she needed presence of mind, and she had it. In the evening the whole company stopped at some swamp, and at dawn they set out again. This time, in order to see better, the traveler held herself with her back upward and her head forward.

The ducks flew over harvested fields, yellow forests, and villages full of grain stacks. The sounds of human voices and the rhythm of flails threshing rye drifted upward from below. People looked at the flock, noticed something strange among the ducks, and pointed their fingers.

The Frog longed desperately to fly lower, to show herself, and to hear what people were saying. At the next halt she said:

"Could we not fly a little lower? The height makes me dizzy, and I am afraid I may fall if I suddenly feel unwell."

The good ducks promised to fly lower. The next day they flew so low that they could hear voices.

"Look, look!" cried the children in one village. "The ducks are carrying a frog!"

The Frog heard this, and her heart leaped.

"Look, look!" shouted the grown-ups in another village. "What a wonder!"

"Do they know it was I who thought of this, and not the ducks?" the Frog wondered.

"Look, look!" cried people in a third village. "What a marvel! Who ever invented such a clever trick?"

This was too much for the Frog. Forgetting all caution, she shouted with all her might:

"It was I! I! I!"

With that cry she turned over in the air and fell to the ground. The ducks cried out loudly. One of them tried to catch their poor companion on the way down, but missed. The Frog, kicking all four legs, fell rapidly. But since the ducks were flying quickly, she did not fall onto the hard road beneath the place where she had cried out. Instead, by great good luck, she splashed into a muddy pond on the edge of the village.

She soon came to the surface and at once shouted excitedly again:

"It was I! It was I who thought of it!"

But there was no one around to hear her. The local frogs had hidden in the water in fright at the sudden splash. When they finally began to show themselves again, they stared in amazement at the newcomer.

And she told them the wonderful story of how she had always been thinking, and at last had invented a new and extraordinary way of traveling on ducks. She told how she had her very own ducks, who carried her wherever she wished; how she had visited the beautiful South, where everything was so pleasant, the swamps so warm, and the mosquitoes and other edible insects so abundant.

"I dropped in to see how you live," she said. "I shall stay with you until spring, until my ducks come back for me. I let them go ahead."

But the ducks never came back. They thought that the poor frog had been killed when she fell, and they were very sorry for her.

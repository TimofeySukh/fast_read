Once upon a time there lived a Frog. She lived in a swamp, caught midges and mosquitoes, and croaked loudly with her friends in spring. She might have lived out her whole life in comfort if a stork had not one day happened to notice her. But something quite different took place.

One rainy day the Frog was sitting on a snag that stuck out of the water and enjoying the warm, gentle shower.

"What a lovely, wet day! How wonderful it is to be alive!" she thought.

The rain drummed softly on her speckled, glossy back, and the drops trickled under her belly and along her legs. It felt so pleasant that she almost croaked with delight. Luckily, she remembered that it was already autumn, and frogs are not supposed to croak in autumn. That is what spring is for.

Suddenly she heard a thin, whistling, broken sound high above her. There is a kind of wild duck whose wings sing when they cut through the air, or rather whistle. When a flock of such ducks flies high overhead, the air seems to cry out: "Fyu-fyu-fyu-fyu," though the birds themselves are hardly visible. This time the ducks described a wide semicircle, descended, and landed in the very swamp where the Frog lived.

"Quack, quack," said one of them. "We still have a long way to go. We must have something to eat."

The Frog hid at once. Although she knew that ducks would not eat a large, plump frog like her, she still dived under the snag just in case. But after thinking it over, she decided to raise her wide-eyed head out of the water. She very much wanted to know where the ducks were going.

"Quack, quack," said another duck. "It is getting cold. South, and quickly! South, quickly!"

And all the ducks quacked loudly in approval.

"Ladies," the Frog ventured to say, "what is this South that you are flying to? Forgive me for disturbing you."

The ducks gathered around the Frog. At first they felt like eating her, but each of them decided that she was far too large to swallow. So instead they all began to shout and flap their wings.

"It is wonderful in the South! It is warm there now. There are such lovely warm swamps there, and such delicious worms. It is wonderful in the South!"

They shrieked so loudly that they nearly deafened the Frog. At last she persuaded them to be quiet and asked one of them, the fattest and wisest-looking duck, to explain what the South was like. When the duck told her about it, the Frog was thrilled, but being cautious by nature she still asked:

"And are there many midges and mosquitoes there?"

"Oh, clouds of them!" answered the duck.

"Croak!" cried the Frog, and at once looked around to make sure none of her friends had heard her croak in autumn. She could not help herself. "Take me with you!"

"That is astonishing!" cried the duck. "How can we take you? You have no wings."

"When are you leaving?" asked the Frog.

"Soon, very soon!" cried all the ducks together. "Quack, quack! It is cold here. To the South! To the South!"

"Give me only five minutes to think," said the Frog. "I shall come back at once. I am sure I can think of something good."

She splashed down into the water from the branch on which she had climbed back up, buried herself in the mud, and hid there completely so that nothing around her would distract her thoughts.

Five minutes passed. The ducks were already about to fly away when suddenly the Frog's face appeared above the water beside the snag. It was glowing with the brightest expression a frog can possibly wear.

"I have thought of it! I have found a way!" she cried. "Let two of you take a small stick in your beaks, and I shall hold onto the middle with my mouth. You will fly and I will ride. The only thing is that you must not quack, and I must not croak. Then everything will go beautifully."

Carrying even a light frog for thousands of miles while keeping silent was hardly a pleasure, but the ducks were so amazed by her cleverness that they all agreed.

They decided to change places every two hours. Since there were many ducks and only one frog, no duck would have to carry her too often. They found a strong, straight stick. Two ducks took it in their beaks, the Frog clasped the middle in her mouth, and the whole flock rose into the air.

After this, I said, compare our nature, in respect of education and its lack, to the following condition. Imagine human beings living in an underground dwelling like a cave, with an entrance open to the light and stretching along the whole length of the cave. They have been there from childhood, with chains on their legs and necks, so that they remain in the same place and can look only straight ahead, because the chains prevent them from turning their heads. Light reaches them from a fire burning high and far behind them. Between the fire and the prisoners there is a road above them, and along this road a low wall has been built, like the screen that puppet-handlers set in front of the people over which they show their puppets.

Then imagine that along this wall people are carrying all kinds of artifacts: statues of men and other living creatures made of stone, wood, and every sort of material. As you would expect, some of the carriers are speaking and some are silent.

Such prisoners would see nothing of themselves or one another except the shadows cast by the fire on the wall of the cave facing them.

And if they could talk to one another, would they not think that the names they used applied to the things they could see before them?

Of course.

And if their prison had an echo coming from the wall opposite them, whenever one of the passers-by spoke, would they not suppose that the voice belonged to the passing shadow?

Necessarily.

Then in every way such prisoners would believe that the truth is nothing other than the shadows of those artifacts.

Consider, then, what being released from their chains and cured of their ignorance would naturally be like. Whenever one of them was freed, compelled to stand up suddenly, turn his neck around, walk, and look up toward the light, all these actions would be painful. Because of the glare he would be unable to see the things whose shadows he had seen before.

He would need time to get used to seeing things above. At first he would most easily see shadows, then reflections of people and things in water, and only later the things themselves. After that he could look at what is in the heavens and the heavens themselves more easily at night, looking at the light of the stars and the moon, than by day at the sun and its light.

Finally, I suppose, he would be able to see the sun itself, not reflections of it in water or in any alien place, but the sun just as it is.

Necessarily.

And after that he would reason that the sun provides the seasons and the years, governs everything in the visible realm, and is in some way the cause of all the things that he and his companions used to see.

Clearly.

Then, if he remembered his first dwelling place, the wisdom there, and his fellow prisoners, do you not think that he would count himself happy for the change and feel pity for them?

Certainly.

And if there had been honors and praises among them for whoever was sharpest at observing the passing shadows and best at remembering which came before, which after, and which together, and from these was most able to predict what would come next, do you think that the man who had been freed would desire those prizes or envy those honored there? Rather, he would prefer to endure anything rather than share their opinions and live as they do.

Yes, he said, I think he would choose to suffer anything rather than live that way.

Now consider this too. If such a person were to go down again and sit in the same seat, would not his eyes, coming suddenly out of the sunlight, be filled with darkness?

Very much so.

And if he once more had to compete with those perpetual prisoners in judging the shadows, before his eyes had recovered and adjusted, would he not make himself ridiculous? Would they not say that he returned from his ascent with ruined eyesight, and that it is not even worth trying to go upward? And if they could somehow seize the one who tried to free them and lead them upward, would they not kill him?

They certainly would.

This whole image, dear Glaucon, must be fitted together with what was said before. The visible realm should be compared to the prison dwelling, and the fire inside it to the power of the sun. The upward journey and the contemplation of the things above should be taken as the ascent of the soul to the intelligible realm.
